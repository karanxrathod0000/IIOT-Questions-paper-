Q1 (Option A)

Architecture Components & WSN

a) Describe the functions of: i) Sensors, ii) Gateways, iii) Routers 6 Marks

  • i) Sensors (The Perception Layer): The "eyes and ears" of IIoT.
    Function: Transduction (Physical → Electrical). They detect changes in environment (Temp, Pressure, Vibration).
    Role: Provide the raw data foundation. Example: Piezoelectric sensors for motor vibration analysis.
  • ii) Gateways (The Bridge): Connects OT (Operational Tech) to IT (Information Tech).
    Function: Protocol Translation (e.g., Modbus/Zigbee → MQTT/HTTP), Data Aggregation (filtering noise), and basic Security (Encryption).
    Role: Decouples the edge devices from the cloud, saving bandwidth.
  • iii) Routers (The Network Layer): Directs traffic across networks.
    Function: Packet switching based on IP addresses. Manages WAN connectivity (4G/Fiber).
    Role: Ensures secure, reliable data paths between the factory LAN and the Cloud/Internet.

b) What is a cloud broker and is it used in IIoT? 6 Marks

Definition: A cloud broker is a third-party entity or middleware that manages the use, performance, and delivery of cloud services. It negotiates relationships between cloud providers (AWS, Azure) and cloud consumers (The Factory).

Is it used in IIoT? YES.

It is crucial for Multi-Cloud Strategies where a company might use AWS for storage and Azure for AI. The broker handles:

  • Service Intermediation: Adding value (e.g., unified identity management) on top of raw services.
  • Service Aggregation: Combining services from multiple vendors into one dashboard.
  • Service Arbitrage: Dynamically switching providers for cost/performance.

c) How can WSNs be used to collect data from industrial environments? 6 Marks

Wireless Sensor Networks (WSN): Distributed autonomous sensors (nodes) that monitor physical conditions and pass data wirelessly to a gateway.

Applications
  • Rotating Machinery: Monitoring motors/turbines where wires would break.
  • Hazardous Areas: Chemical tanks or nuclear sites where cabling is dangerous.
  • Large Scale: Pipelines or Agriculture (LoRaWAN).
Advantages
  • Low installation cost (No wires).
  • Scalability (Mesh topology).
  • Flexibility in deployment.
Q2 (Option B)

Modems, Transducers & Data Processing

a) Describe functions of: i) Modems, ii) Cloud brokers, iii) Servers 6 Marks

  • i) Modems (Modulator-Demodulator): Converts digital data to analog for transmission over phone/radio lines. In IIoT, Cellular Modems (LTE/5G/NB-IoT) are vital for connecting remote assets (e.g., wind turbines) where no Ethernet exists.
  • ii) Cloud Brokers: (See Q1b). They act as intermediaries to simplify the management of complex, multi-vendor cloud environments, providing a single point of entry and billing.
  • iii) Servers (The Brains): High-performance computing units.
    - Edge Servers: Process data locally for low latency (robotic control).
    - Cloud Servers: Store massive historical data and run AI training models.

b) Explain the difference between a sensor and a transducer 6 Marks

Feature Transducer Sensor
Definition Device converting energy from one form to another. Device detecting a physical parameter and converting it to a signal.
Scope Broad category (includes actuators). Sub-category of transducers (Input only).
Examples Loudspeaker (Elec → Sound), Motor. Thermocouple (Heat → Elec), Mic.

"All sensors are transducers, but not all transducers are sensors."

c) Importance of data filtering and aggregation at the IIoT layer 6 Marks

Industrial machines generate massive raw data (Big Data). Sending it all to the cloud is inefficient.

  • Bandwidth Efficiency: Reduces cost by not sending noise (e.g., "temperature steady" 1000 times/sec). Reduces data volume by up to 90%.
  • Latency Reduction: Enables immediate reaction at the Edge (e.g., emergency stop) without waiting for cloud round-trip.
  • Data Quality: Aggregation (e.g., 1-minute averages) creates cleaner datasets for analytics.

b) Process of designing and developing a digital twin 6 Marks

  1. Define: Identify the physical asset and the business goal (e.g., "Predict failure of Turbine X").
  2. Model Construction: Create a virtual representation. Can be Physics-based (CAD/Stress analysis) or Data-driven (Machine Learning).
  3. Data Integration: Instrument the physical asset with sensors and map these real-time streams to the virtual model's parameters.
  4. Validation: Ensure the twin behaves exactly like the real asset under known conditions.
  5. Simulation: Use the verified twin to run "What-If" scenarios (e.g., "What if load increases 20%?").

Platform Selection, Twins & Security

a) Key factors to consider when choosing an IIoT cloud platform 6 Marks

  • Scalability: Can it handle 100 devices today and 1 million tomorrow? Auto-scaling capabilities.
  • Interoperability: Does it support open standards (MQTT, OPC-UA) or is it a "Walled Garden"?

b) Challenges and benefits of using IIoT cloud for digital twin 6 Marks

Benefits
  • Compute Power: Cloud offers infinite resources for complex simulations.
  • Storage: Stores petabytes of history for model training.
  • Collaboration: Global access for remote experts.
Challenges
  • Latency: Network lag delays synchronization (Twin lags reality).
  • Data Cost: Uploading high-frequency data is expensive.
  • IP Risk: Putting proprietary CAD models on public cloud.

c) Security and privacy challenges associated with IIoT cloud platforms 6 Marks

  • Data Breaches: Centralized cloud storage is a prime target for hackers to steal proprietary manufacturing secrets.
  • Multi-Tenancy Risks: In public clouds, your data sits on same hardware as others. Risk of side-channel attacks.
  • Insecure APIs: Weak interfaces are the most common entry point for attacks.
  • Compliance/Residency: Legal requirement to keep data within country borders (Data Sovereignty).
Q5 (Option A)

Security: Integrity & Identity

a) Compare and contrast message integrity protection mechanisms 9 Marks

Integrity ensures data is not altered in transit.

Mechanism How it works Pros/Cons for IIoT
Checksums (CRC) Mathematical sum of bits. Pros: Fast, good for noise.
Cons: NOT secure (Easy to forge).
MAC (Message Authentication Code) Hash function + Secret Key (HMAC). Pros: Verifies Integrity AND Authenticity.
Cons: Key management (Need shared key).
Digital Signatures Asymmetric Crypto (Private Key signs, Public verifies). Pros: Non-Repudiation (Highest Security).
Cons: High CPU usage for small sensors.

b) Select and implement identity establishment mechanism (Smart Meter) 8 Marks

Scenario: A Smart Meter connecting to a Utility Grid.

Selected Mechanism: Public Key Infrastructure (PKI) using X.509 Certificates.

Implementation Steps:
  1. Manufacturing: The Utility (Certificate Authority) burns a unique Private Key and a signed Digital Certificate into the meter's secure chip (TPM).
  2. Deployment: Meter is installed.
  3. Handshake: Meter connects to server and presents its Certificate.
  4. Verification: Server checks the digital signature against the CA. If valid, it knows the device is genuine.
  5. Result: Rogue devices cannot connect because they lack a signed certificate.
Q6 (Option B)

Security Definitions & Integrity

a) Define: i) Identity Establishment, ii) Access Control 9 Marks

i) Identity Establishment (Authentication)

The process of verifying the claim of a subject (user or device) to a specific identity. It answers the question "Who are you?". In IIoT, this is done via Certificates, API Keys, or Passwords.

ii) Access Control (Authorization)

The process of determining what an authenticated entity is allowed to do. It answers the question "What can you do?". Example: An 'Admin' can update firmware, but a 'Viewer' can only read data. Techniques include RBAC (Role-Based) and ABAC (Attribute-Based).

b) Describe how to ensure the integrity of messages in a given IIoT system 8 Marks

Core Technique: Cryptographic Hashing (e.g., SHA-256)

Process flow:

  1. Sender: Takes the message $M$ and a secret key $K$.
  2. Calculation: Calculates Hash $H = HMAC(M, K)$.
  3. Transmission: Sends Message $M$ and Hash $H$ to receiver.
  4. Receiver: Receives $M'$ and $H'$. Calculates its own hash $H_{new} = HMAC(M', K)$.
  5. Verification: Compares $H_{new}$ with $H'$. If they match, the message was not altered. If they differ, the message is discarded as corrupt/tampered.

Q2 (Option B)

IIAF & Architecture Layers

a) Discuss Industrial Internet Architecture Framework (IIAF) 10 Marks

Purpose: Created by IIC (Industrial Internet Consortium) to provide a standard methodology for designing IIoT systems, ensuring interoperability between vendors.

Key Principles (The Viewpoints):

  • Business Viewpoint: Focuses on stakeholders and business vision/values. (Why are we doing this?)
  • Usage Viewpoint: Describes user activities and system usage sequences. (How is it used?)
  • Functional Viewpoint: Break down into functional blocks (Control, Operations, Information). (What does it do?)
  • Implementation Viewpoint: The physical technologies and components used. (What is it built of?)

It guides design by ensuring all aspects (Business to Tech) are aligned.

b) Discuss Layers of IIoT (Sensing, Processing, Comm, Networking) 8 Marks

1. IIoT Sensing Layer Identification and data collection. Includes Sensors (Temperature, RFID) and Actuators. The "Skin" of the system.
2. IIoT Processing Layer (Edge/Fog) Middleware layer. Filters raw data to remove noise, converts formats, and makes local decisions (e.g., emergency stop). Reduces latency.
3. IIoT Communication Layer Defines how devices talk. Includes protocols like MQTT (Publish/Subscribe), CoAP (Restricted Web), and OPC-UA (Machine Data).
4. IIoT Networking Layer The infrastructure transporting the bits. Includes 4G/5G, Wi-Fi 6, Ethernet, and Routers/Switches handling IP addressing.

Security Importance & Management

a) Importance of security in IIoT and consequences of breaches 10 Marks

Importance: Unlike standard IT, IIoT interacts with the physical world. Security is not just about data privacy; it's about Safety.

Consequences of Breaches:

  • Physical Safety: Hackers overriding safety controls in a chemical plant could cause explosions or injury.
  • Operational Downtime: Ransomware (e.g., LockerGoga) can brick controllers, stopping production for weeks.
  • Environmental Damage: Opening valves in a water treatment plant or pipeline.
  • IP Theft: Stealing proprietary formulas or manufacturing processes.

b) Management aspects of cybersecurity and stakeholders 7 Marks

Cybersecurity is a process, not a product. It requires:

  • Risk Assessment: Identifying critical assets and vulnerabilities.
  • Defense in Depth: Multiple layers of security (Physical, Network, Application).

Stakeholders:

  • OT Engineers: Understand the machine physics and safety constraints.
  • IT Security Admins: Understand network defense, firewalls, and patching.
  • Management/C-Suite: Must provide budget and enforce policy compliance.
Q6 (Option B)

Access Control & Identity

a) Access control in IIoT, mechanisms and techniques 10 Marks

Concept: Restricting what authorized users/devices can do.

Mechanisms:

  • RBAC (Role-Based Access Control): Assigns permissions to roles (Admin, Operator, Viewer). Users are assigned roles. Best for manageability.
  • ABAC (Attribute-Based Access Control): More dynamic. Grants access based on attributes (Time of day, User Location, Device Type). e.g., "Allow remote control only if Operator is on-site".
  • Network ACLs: Firewall rules allowing specific IP addresses to talk to specific ports (White-listing).

b) Importance of identity establishment and protocols 7 Marks

Importance: If you don't know who is connecting, you can't enforce security. "Spoofing" involves a hacker pretending to be a sensor to inject bad data.

Methods/Protocols:

  • X.509 Certificates (PKI): The gold standard. Digital IDs signed by a trusted authority.
  • TLS (Transport Layer Security): Uses certificates to create an encrypted tunnel. Mutual TLS (mTLS) authenticates both server and device.
  • OAuth 2.0 / OIDC: Modern token-based authentication for users and APIs.
